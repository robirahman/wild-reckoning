\## üêîüêñ What conditions are typically like in factory farms



Across academic, NGO, and government reviews, factory farming (also called \*intensive livestock production\* or \*concentrated animal feeding operations\*) has several defining characteristics:



\### 1. Extreme confinement



Animals are kept at very high stocking densities to maximize production efficiency.



\* Chickens may live in sheds holding \*\*tens of thousands of birds\*\*.

\* Egg-laying hens are often confined in \*\*battery cages\*\* so small they cannot fully stretch their wings or walk normally.

\* Pregnant pigs are frequently confined in \*\*gestation crates\*\* so narrow they cannot turn around. (\[PMC]\[1])



Factory systems are explicitly designed around productivity rather than natural behavior.



---



\### 2. Prevention of natural behaviors



A central welfare issue identified across reports is behavioral deprivation:



\* Chickens cannot perch, dust-bathe, forage, or nest.

\* Pigs cannot root, explore, or build nests.

\* Animals are often unable to form stable social groups.



Large global reviews note that factory-farmed animals are ‚Äúpacked into barns and cages‚Ä¶with little room to move,‚Äù preventing exploration, play, and maternal behavior. (\[World Animal Protection]\[2])



---



\### 3. Rapid growth and physical suffering (especially chickens)



Modern broiler chickens are selectively bred to grow extremely fast.



Typical consequences documented in welfare literature include:



\* Lameness and joint failure

\* Heart and lung problems

\* Skin lesions from sitting on manure-covered flooring

\* Difficulty walking or standing near slaughter age



Broilers may be raised from hatch to slaughter in \*\*3‚Äì12 weeks\*\*, often resting on feces-laden litter that causes burns and infections. (\[sustainability.emory.edu]\[3])



---



\### 4. Painful procedures without anesthesia



Common industry practices include:



\* \*\*Beak trimming\*\* in chickens to reduce stress-induced pecking

\* \*\*Tail docking\*\* or teeth clipping in pigs

\* Castration in some systems



These are frequently performed without pain relief. (\[sustainability.emory.edu]\[3])



---



\### 5. Disease pressure and antibiotic use



Because animals are crowded indoors:



\* Waste accumulation increases ammonia and dust.

\* Respiratory disease spreads easily.

\* Immune systems are stressed.



Reports link intensive confinement systems to routine antibiotic use intended to prevent outbreaks rather than treat illness. (\[bbfaw.com]\[4])



---



\### 6. Psychological stress



Crowding and confinement can produce abnormal behaviors:



\* Feather pecking and cannibalism among chickens

\* Bar biting and repetitive movements in pigs

\* Learned helplessness or inactivity



Such behaviors are widely used as indicators of poor welfare in animal-science research.
